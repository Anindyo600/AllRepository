The Black Death (1347–1351)

The Black Death was one of the deadliest pandemics in history, killing an estimated 75–200 million people worldwide.
it was caused by the Yersinia pestis bacterium, which spread through fleas carried by rats.
The disease spread rapidly across Europe, Asia, and North Africa, wiping out up to 60% of Europe's population.
Symptoms included fever, swollen lymph nodes (buboes), vomiting, and blackened skin due to internal bleeding.
The high death toll led to severe labor shortages, weakening the feudal system and increasing wages for peasants.
Many people believed the plague was a punishment from God, leading to mass hysteria and persecution of minorities like Jews.
The Black Death had long-term effects on society, economy, and medicine, leading to advancements in public health.

World War II (1939–1945)

World War II was the deadliest conflict in human history, with an estimated 70–85 million deaths.
It was fought between the Allies (USA, USSR, UK, China, etc.) and the Axis Powers (Germany, Japan, Italy, etc.).
The war included major battles like Stalingrad, D-Day, and Midway, along with devastating air raids.
Adolf Hitler's Nazi regime carried out the Holocaust, which resulted in the genocide of six million Jews and millions of others.
The war ended with the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, forcing Japan to surrender in August 1945.
The aftermath led to the creation of the United Nations (UN) and the beginning of the Cold War between the USA and USSR.
World War II changed global politics, leading to the decline of European empires and the rise of the USA and USSR as superpowers.
